Module 05

The Mechanics of the Close

1

The Power of the Pause

"After you ask, 'Between $0 and my price, what can you actually afford?' — YOU MUST WAIT. The first person to speak usually loses."

2

The "Fairness" Trigger

Use the word "Fair." Most people find it difficult to say "No, that's not fair" to a reasonable explanation of business costs. "I can't do $15k, but I can do $17k so my guys get paid and we deliver a safe project. Is that fair?"

3

Stakeholder Control

Never let a "messenger" sell for you. If a manager says "I need to talk to the owner," insist on being part of that meeting "so nothing gets lost in translation." "I don't want to make you play telephone with the engineering specs. Let's set up a quick 5-minute intro with the owner so I can answer the technical questions directly."

The Receptionist Script

Mastering the first 15 seconds to bypass the gatekeeper.

"Hey, I'm Raphael with Mainstream Electric. I'm working with businesses in [Area] on their backup power and outage protection. → [Physical Interrupt: Hand them your card] "Here's my card just so you have it." We're seeing a lot of local businesses getting caught completely offline during these recent PSPS events. Who handles your facility operations or emergency preparedness?"

Why this works:

  • The Physical Interrupt Handing them an object mid-sentence breaks their auto-rejection reflex. It proves you are a real, local professional, not a solicitor, building immediate trust before you ever ask for a name.
  • "PSPS events" Real, fresh pain point. Proves you are local and relevant.
  • "Emergency preparedness" Frames the conversation around risk mitigation, not a luxury purchase.
  • "Who handles" Secures the right name and title without aggressively demanding access.

The Drop-Off / Follow-Up

How to gracefully exit while leaving the door wide open. Choose the script that fits their specific objection.

Scenario 1: The "Emergency Backup" (Standard)
"No worries at all. → [Physical Interrupt: Hand them brochure] "I'll just leave this risk-assessment checklist for your operations team." By the way, if you guys are ever looking at upgrading these older lights to LEDs, SMUD is actually covering up to half the cost right now. Do you know if anyone here is already grabbing that free money before it dries up?"
Scenario 2: The "We Just Lease" Pivot
"Makes sense. Usually, the landlord handles the building, but the tenant takes the hit on revenue when power drops. → [Physical Interrupt: Hand them brochure] "I'll leave this packet for your manager—it actually outlines how tenants are using SMUD rebates to get new LED lighting." Why keep burning your own revenue on high energy bills just because the landlord won't upgrade the fixtures? I'll leave my card in case your manager wants to run those numbers."
Scenario 3: The "Micro-Commitment" (Highly Dismissive)
"I completely understand you're swamped today. → [Physical Interrupt: Hand them brochure] "I'll just leave this quick one-pager on local outage protection for the manager's desk." By the way, if they're tired of paying high energy bills for this older lighting, we do SMUD-subsidized LED retrofits too. When is typically a better time for me to pop my head back in for just 5 minutes—later this Thursday, or early next week?"
Scenario 4: The "Specific Asset" Hook

"No problem. I noticed the [Specific Asset], so I know downtime is expensive for you guys. → [Physical Interrupt: Hand them brochure] "I'll leave this info on battery backups for whoever manages that equipment." By the way, if you guys ever want to upgrade your [Lighting Type], SMUD covers up to half the cost and it drastically cuts overhead. Here's my card, just in case."

Asset & LED Cheat Sheet:
  • Restaurants: [Walk-in freezers / POS systems] → [Kitchen task lighting / Dining area ambiance]
  • Dealerships: [Service bay lifts / Security gates] → [Showroom floor pop / Outdoor lot security]
  • Retail Stores: [POS registers / Inventory systems] → [Product display lighting / High-bay floor lights]
  • Offices/Consulting: [Server rooms / Network closets] → [Removing fluorescent glare / Modernizing the office]

Why this works:

  • 1.
    The Double Pattern Interrupt Handing them the brochure pauses their rejection. Pivoting to "saving money on lighting" right after changes the subject entirely, disarming their final defense.
  • 2.
    Positions you as the "Backup" Acknowledging they might have a current electrician, but positioning yourself as the emergency backup, removes the pressure to "switch" providers today.
  • 3.
    The FOMO Question (Scenario 1) Asking if they are grabbing the "free money before it dries up" triggers intense Fear Of Missing Out. If the receptionist is the true DM, they'll stop you to ask for details. If they aren't, it creates urgency to hand the brochure to the boss immediately.

The Walkthrough Ask

Transitioning from the introduction to getting physical access to the electrical room.

"Hey [Name], Raphael with Mainstream. We're doing some backup power upgrades nearby. Mind if I take 10 minutes to grab some photos of your main service panel and critical loads? My engineering team will map out exactly what it takes to keep you online—if the numbers make sense, great. If not, at least you know your options."

The Psychology:

  • "10 minutes to grab some photos" Lowers the perceived time commitment. You aren't asking for an hour-long meeting, you just need a few photos.
  • "My engineering team..." Elevates your status. You aren't just an electrician quoting a job; you are the field liaison for an entire engineering department.
  • The Binary Choice Close Instead of asking "Can I take photos?" (Yes/No), ask "Should I start with the main panel or the backup unit?" This assumes the 'Yes' and gives them a choice of *where* to start, not *if* you can start.

Gatekeeper Workarounds

1. The "Wrong Department" Shuffle

Sound like an inspector or high-level tech.

"Maybe you can help me—I might have the wrong department. I'm looking for the person who handles the physical plant or electrical infrastructure. Is that still under [Name]?"

2. The "Inbound/Outbound" Trick

Loading dock guys bypass receptionists.

"I was looking for the Maintenance Lead, but the line was busy. Is there a direct extension for the Loading Dock or Service Entrance?"

Tier-Down Pivots

Pivot to Service Account
THEY SAY: "We already have an electrician."

"Glad you've got someone. Out of curiosity, are they handling your PM on a contract basis, or do you call them as-needed?"

"Most of our clients save 30% by switching to a preventive contract rather than paying $250/hr emergency rates. Would it make sense to have us on file as your secondary backup?"

Pivot to LED Retrofit
THEY SAY: "We don't have budget for backup power."

"Totally understand. Since I'm here, I noticed your lighting. An LED retrofit cuts energy costs by 60%, and SMUD rebates cover up to 50% upfront. It basically pays for itself in 18 months—worth at least running the numbers?"

Rapid-Fire Objections

"We already have a generator."
Hint: Age dictates code compliance.

"Smart. When was it installed? The average commercial lifespan is 15-20 years, and older units don't always meet current NEC code for ATS integration. Does it cover full production load or just life safety?"

"We don't own the building."
Hint: Revenue loss is on the tenant, not the landlord.

"Got it. Does your lease include SLA guarantees for uptime? If the grid drops, the landlord isn't covering your revenue loss. Most businesses install their own BESS as a continuity investment, not a building mod."

"We've never had a long outage."
Hint: Make them do the math.

"That's good—but the average downtime event in CA is 4-6 hours. For a facility this size, what does ONE hour of downtime cost you in lost production or spoiled inventory?"

"What about solar?"
Hint: Solar without storage dies in an outage.

"Solar panels shut down when the grid goes offline unless you have battery storage (BESS). BESS is the core need for outage protection; solar is the add-on."

The "6-Month Regret" Mechanics

Instead of asking "What's broken?", ask:
"What happens to your revenue if this system fails during your busiest week 6 months from now?"

This question isn't just a filter; it’s a time-travel tool. It shifts the conversation from a $500 repair today to a $50,000 risk mitigation project. Always hunt for the root cause—if a breaker trips, is it the breaker, or did they add new machinery? Fixing the root cause leads to a system upgrade proposal.

The Boss's Data Packet

Your job in the field isn't to size the complex ATS project—it's to capture the exact data your boss needs to build an airtight ROI proposal.

Cost of Failure Missing one nameplate photo means a second site visit or a delayed quote. Complete data = faster engineering = higher close rates.

Panel Nameplates

Capture the manufacturer, voltage, phase, and main breaker amperage. Make sure the text is completely legible. If the panel is old or degraded, take multiple angles. The engineering team needs to know exactly what they are tying the ATS into.

Circuit Schedules

Photograph the panel directory. Note which specific breakers are currently tied to critical operations (servers, refrigeration, assembly lines). If the schedule is illegible or missing, note that—it’s an easy add-on service to trace and relabel the panel.

Equipment Layout

Take wide shots of the electrical room and exterior walls. We need to know if there is physical clearance for a new ATS, BESS system, or outdoor generator pad. Note any potential obstacles like piping, HVAC units, or tight clearances.

Existing Violations

Keep an eye out for obvious NEC violations: blocked panel clearances, double-tapped breakers, or ungrounded systems. Documenting these allows us to bundle code-compliance upgrades into the main backup power proposal to sweeten the ROI.
