Module 02

The 2-Minute Pain Audit

Build the pain before you sell the solution. This audit uncovers their true cost of downtime and justifies the site walk.

Two Paths to the Site Walk

  • A
    Direct Approach (Confident Decision Makers) When the decision maker is confident and engaged, skip the audit and go straight for the site walk: "We're doing backup power nearby. Mind if I grab some photos of your panel?"
  • B
    Pain Audit First (Hesitant or Skeptical) When they're hesitant, busy, or skeptical, run the 2-Minute Pain Audit first. Once they quantify the problem ($50K revenue loss), asking for 10 minutes to photograph the panel feels natural.

Why the Pain Audit Works

  • 1.
    Makes Them Do the Math When they write down "$50,000" next to "Revenue Hemorrhage," they're now emotionally invested in solving a $50K problem—not buying a $25K generator.
  • 2.
    Pre-Qualifies the Tier Their answers tell you if they need Tier 1 (Backup Power), Tier 2 (Service Account), or Tier 3 (LED Retrofit). You aren't guessing anymore.
  • 3.
    Justifies the Site Walk After they check 4-5 boxes, asking for "10 minutes to photograph the electrical room" feels like the obvious next step—not an intrusive request.

Read the Room: Which Path?

  • Use Path A (Direct): Decision maker is engaged, facility clearly has downtime risk (restaurants, manufacturing, cold storage), they're nodding along to your opener.
  • Use Path B (Audit First): They're hesitant, "we've never had issues," skeptical body language, or you're talking to a gatekeeper who needs ammunition for their boss.
  • Don't do this at the front desk: Receptionists don't have authority or data. Get to the decision maker first.

Module 05

The Mechanics of the Close

1

The Power of the Pause

"After you ask, 'Between $0 and my price, what can you actually afford?' — YOU MUST WAIT. The first person to speak usually loses."

2

The "Fairness" Trigger

Use the word "Fair." Most people find it difficult to say "No, that's not fair" to a reasonable explanation of business costs. "I can't do $15k, but I can do $17k so my guys get paid and we deliver a safe project. Is that fair?"

3

Stakeholder Control

Never let a "messenger" sell for you. If a manager says "I need to talk to the owner," insist on being part of that meeting "so nothing gets lost in translation." "I don't want to make you play telephone with the engineering specs. Let's set up a quick 5-minute intro with the owner so I can answer the technical questions directly."

Why this works:

The Introduction Script

My engineering team will map out exactly what it takes to keep you online—if the numbers make sense, great. If not, at least you know your options."

The Psychology:

  • "10 minutes to grab some photos" Lowers the perceived time commitment. You aren't asking for an hour-long meeting, you just need a few photos.
  • "My engineering team..." Elevates your status. You aren't just an electrician quoting a job; you are the field liaison for an entire engineering department.
  • "If the numbers make sense, great. If not..." Removes pressure. You're giving them an out, which paradoxically makes them more likely to say yes.
The Binary Choice Close:

"Should I start by taking photos of the main service panel, or is there a backup unit I should document first?"

Instead of asking "Can I take photos?" (Yes/No), you assume the 'Yes' and give them a choice of WHERE to start, not IF you can start.

"Have sudden power drops or harsh surges ever damaged your expensive servers, HVAC systems, or critical manufacturing machinery?"

→ Identifies hard asset damage beyond revenue loss.

The "False Hope" Failure

"Has your backup generator ever failed to start exactly when you needed it most, leaving you completely stranded?"

→ If YES: Instant Tier 2 (Service Account) qualification. They need quarterly PM contracts.

Q5
Inventory & Data Spoilage

"Are you at risk of losing perishable inventory, sensitive cloud data, or active production runs during an unannounced shutoff?"

→ Restaurants, cold storage, data centers = high-value targets.

Q6
Reputation Damage

"Have clients, tenants, or partners lost trust in your facility's reliability because you couldn't deliver during an outage?"

→ The emotional/relationship cost that's harder to quantify but deeply felt.

Q7
Compliance & Safety Nightmares

"Would an extended outage put you in violation of health codes, safety regulations, or expose you to severe compliance fines?"

→ Medical facilities, food service, labs = regulatory risk layer.

  • 1.
    No Threat Positioning You aren't asking them to fire their current electrician. You're acknowledging that relationship while positioning yourself as the safety net.
  • 2.

  • 3.
    Specialize Where They Don't Most general electricians avoid backup power projects (too complex) or LED retrofits (too time-consuming). This is your opening to carve out specialized work.
Follow-Up Question to Secure the Backup Role:

"If your current electrician ever gets backed up during a busy week or you need a second opinion on a quote, can I leave my card so you have it on file?"

Closing Strategy 1: The Trial Run

Low commitment, prove value first. Perfect for risk-averse managers who need to see results before signing a contract.

"I totally understand you want to see how we work before committing to a full contract. Let's start with a one-time PM visit—we'll do a full electrical inspection, test all your critical systems, and give you a detailed report on anything that needs attention. If you like the work and the level of detail we provide, we can talk about setting up a quarterly contract. Fair?"

Why This Works:

  • Removes Risk: They're only committing to one visit, not a multi-year contract.
  • Demonstrates Value: Your detailed inspection report proves you're more thorough than their current "as-needed" electrician.
  • Uncovers Issues: The inspection almost always finds 2-3 issues they didn't know existed, creating immediate upsell opportunities.
Best For:

Facilities that have never had a contracted electrician, skeptical operations managers, or companies burned by previous contractors.

Closing Strategy 2: Emergency-Only Retainer

Fast response, no trip charges. Perfect for 24/7 operations that can't afford downtime but aren't ready for full PM contracts.

"I completely understand you're not ready for quarterly PM visits. How about this: for $200/month, you get priority emergency response—guaranteed 4-hour callout for critical failures, no trip charges, and you skip the line when we're slammed. You only pay the retainer; we bill labor and materials at our contract rate ($125/hr instead of $250/hr emergency). Does that sound like a better fit?"

Why This Works:

  • Low Monthly Commitment: $200/month feels manageable compared to a $1,500/quarter PM contract.
  • Instant ROI on First Call: First emergency call saves them $500+ in trip charges and emergency rate difference.
  • Priority Positioning: They're now "your client" when an emergency hits, not a one-off service call.
Best For:

24/7 operations (restaurants, gas stations, medical facilities), facilities with older equipment prone to failure, companies with high downtime costs.

Closing Strategy 3: Scope-Specific Contract

Carve out specialized work they don't trust their general electrician to handle. You keep your current electrician for everything else.

"I get that you already have an electrician you trust for general work. What if we just handle your generator maintenance and testing? Most general electricians avoid backup power systems because the liability is high and the training is specialized. We'll do quarterly generator testing, transfer switch inspections, and load bank tests for $1,200/year. You keep your current guy for everything else. Does that make sense?"

Why This Works:

  • No Threat to Existing Relationship: You're not trying to replace their electrician—you're filling a gap.
  • Specialized Expertise: Generator maintenance is complex and most electricians avoid it. You're the expert.
  • Foot in the Door: Once you're doing their generator work, you naturally discover other issues and expand the scope over time.
Best For:

Facilities with backup generators, companies with specialized equipment (UPS systems, EV chargers), or sites that need NEC-compliant testing/documentation.

Photograph the panel directory. Note which specific breakers are currently tied to critical operations (servers, refrigeration, assembly lines). If the schedule is illegible or missing, note that—it's an easy add-on service to trace and relabel the panel.

Equipment Layout

Take wide shots of the electrical room and exterior walls. We need to know if there is physical clearance for a new ATS, BESS system, or outdoor generator pad. Note any potential obstacles like piping, HVAC units, or tight clearances.

Existing Violations

Keep an eye out for obvious NEC violations: blocked panel clearances, double-tapped breakers, or ungrounded systems. Documenting these allows us to bundle code-compliance upgrades into the main backup power proposal to sweeten the ROI.
